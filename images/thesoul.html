What Is a Soul?

The truth is, I don’t know. I have no certain answer, and perhaps there isn’t one.

I remember, once, during our criminalistics class, our professor took us to observe an autopsy. While he turned pale and struggled to stay upright, I felt something closer to disappointment. There was nothing transcendent in what I saw — only the quiet machinery of the body, methodically opened and examined.

In that moment, I understood absence. The body was intact, yet whatever had animated it was gone. It was a house emptied of its tenant, matter stripped of meaning. For a few days afterward, my thoughts and emotions receded. There was no fear in that silence — only a peculiar peace, as if detachment itself were a kind of truth.

On the way home, I bought strawberry ice cream. It was absurdly good, vivid, almost defiant in its simplicity. Perhaps that was my body reasserting itself — reminding me of the immediate, the tangible.

But before leaving that room, I caught myself whispering to the still form, “Don’t worry, you’ll be born again.” I don’t know why I said it. Maybe that impulse — to reach toward something beyond reason — is what we call the soul.

In the years since, I’ve met people with brilliant intellects, their minds alive with precision and logic, yet possessing less soul than that body on the table.
