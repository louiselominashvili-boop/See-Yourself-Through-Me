Winter and the Weight of Inequality

For many people, winter arrives with a sense of wonder: snow-dusted streets, warm lights in windows, festive decorations, and holidays wrapped in comfort. But this version of winter often belongs to those who have the means to enjoy it. For others, the season brings something very different—an ache that settles into everyday life, making even simple tasks feel heavier.

Winter demands warmth, and warmth costs money. Heating a home, keeping feet dry and comfortable, buying thicker clothes, and managing higher utility bills are not optional luxuries; They’re necessities. When someone doesn’t have enough resources to meet those needs, winter becomes a source of constant stress. Waking up to a freezing room isn’t charming. It’s uncomfortable, discouraging, and tiring in a way that accumulates day after day. Even getting dressed in the morning can feel like stepping into a world that wasn’t designed with you in mind.

The contrast becomes especially sharp during the holiday season. While some people fill their homes with decorations, purchase gifts without worry, and treat winter as a time of celebration, others face the reality of not being able to afford presents or festive touches. The holidays, which are supposed to bring joy, can instead highlight what’s out of reach. It’s not that people lack generosity or desire—it’s simply that the basics come first.

At the same time, winter has become a season of luxury for those who can afford it. They glide through snow in large cars equipped with winter tires, wearing high-end coats and curated perfumes, attending holiday events and performances like “The Nutcracker.” Ski trips, mountain resorts, and designer winter gear turn the season into a kind of curated experience. This difference can make winter feel like it’s built for the wealthy, while everyone else is left to navigate its discomforts.

It’s easy to see how these inequalities shape someone’s feelings about winter. If the season only brings cold rooms, rising bills, and reminders of what others can enjoy, it’s natural to fear or resent it. The divide between rich and poor doesn’t just exist in numbers—it shows up in temperatures, wardrobes, holiday traditions, and everyday life.

Winter, then, is not just a change in weather. It becomes a mirror reflecting uneven realities. And for many people, it’s not the snow or the cold that feels harsh—it’s the sense that the season belongs to someone else.
